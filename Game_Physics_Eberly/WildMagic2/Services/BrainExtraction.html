Brain extraction tool.

This algorithm uses an active surfaces algorithm that is analogous to the active contours (or snakes) algorithm used in 2D images. The method is referred to as skull stripping where only those voxels of the brain matter are retained for further processing. An ellipsoid is computed to approximate the brain and is represented as a triangle mesh of elastic material. The center, orientation, and shape of the ellipsoid are based on a histogram analysis of the image. The mesh evolves over time. Surface tension is used to prevent the surface from uncontrolled growth in the directions tangential to the surface. The image data is used to force growth in the normal direction to the surface. The middle slice of a 3D MRI is shown with a slice of the initial ellipsoid. The ellipsoid is a coarse approximation, and the evolution scheme will attempt to modify the ellipsoid to encapsulate the brain matter.



The sequence of images below are from various stages of the surface evolution. The first shows the rendered initial ellipsoid. The last shows the final evolved surface. Initially the surface tension is small, allowing the surface to grow rapidly with possible cusps and self-intersections occurring. This allows you to get relatively close to the brain surface, but rapidly. As time increases, the tension is increased to force the surface not to self-intersect. Once the self-intersections disappear, the tension is reduced to allow a somewhat finer level evolution to reach the brain surface.

























The voxels enclosed by the final surface are colored in shades of cyan in the original image. A few slices of that image are shown below to give you an idea of the accuracy of the segmentation.



The final surface is used as the input to a more sophisticated evolution method that shrink-wraps the gray matter (or white matter if desired).
